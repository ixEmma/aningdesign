A website project moves faster when the client prepares the right decisions and materials before design starts. The designer can then focus on structure, user experience, and visual quality instead of repeatedly stopping to request missing copy, images, or account access.

This website design checklist for clients explains what to prepare and why each item matters. You do not need to design the website yourself. You need to give the designer a reliable foundation.

## Clarify the business goal

Write down the main result the website should support. It may be inquiries, bookings, sales, applications, event registrations, downloads, or clearer business information.

Choose one primary action for each important page. If every action is presented as equally important, visitors may not know what to do. Share how the website fits into the wider business process and what happens after a visitor contacts you.

## Define the target audience

Describe the people the website needs to help. Include their main problem, what they are comparing, what information they need before acting, and common concerns.

Avoid a description as broad as “everyone.” A useful audience note might identify the type of customer, their stage of awareness, location when relevant, budget expectations, or decision criteria. This gives the designer a reason for the page hierarchy and content emphasis.

## Confirm the required pages

List the pages you expect, then review whether each one has a clear purpose. Common pages include Home, About, Services, individual service pages, Projects or Case Studies, Pricing, Blog, FAQ, and Contact.

Also identify policy pages, landing pages, thank-you pages, account pages, or location pages. Tell the designer which existing URLs must remain available during a redesign.

## Prepare the website copy

Collect the content visitors need to understand the business and take action:

- A short explanation of the business
- Service or product details
- The main benefits and practical differences
- About and team information
- Genuine proof, projects, or approved testimonials
- Contact details and opening hours
- Frequently asked questions
- Calls to action

Label drafts clearly and identify who will approve them. If the designer is expected to structure or write copy, include that responsibility in the scope instead of assuming it is part of visual design.

## Organize brand assets

Provide the original logo files, not a screenshot copied from social media. Vector SVG, EPS, AI, or high-resolution transparent PNG files are more useful than a small JPEG.

Share brand colors, fonts, guidelines, icon sets, photography direction, existing templates, and examples of approved materials. Explain which elements are fixed and where the designer has room to improve the system.

If there is no established identity, say so early. Website design cannot create consistent brand recognition from conflicting, unapproved assets.

## Select useful images

Create folders by page or purpose. Use descriptive filenames and identify the priority images. Confirm that you have permission to publish them.

Provide high-quality originals when possible. The designer can resize and optimize an image, but cannot recover detail from a compressed thumbnail. Include team photos, project images, location photography, product images, service context, and approved stock assets.

Do not send hundreds of unfiltered images and expect the designer to guess which ones represent the business accurately.

## List the required features

Separate essential features from later ideas. Examples include contact forms, booking, ecommerce, memberships, gated downloads, search, maps, multilingual content, integrations, calculators, user accounts, or application forms.

For each feature, explain the user action and the expected result. “Add booking” is incomplete without the booking provider, appointment types, schedule rules, confirmation behavior, and account owner.

## Confirm domain, hosting, and account access

Identify who owns the domain, hosting, email, analytics, search tools, CMS, premium plugins, booking system, payment accounts, and other services. Do not create critical business accounts in a contractor’s personal name.

Share access through an appropriate credential manager or invited-user workflow. Record renewal dates and licensing responsibilities. This preparation makes launch and handover safer.

## Share references with reasons

Reference websites are useful when you explain what you like: the navigation, spacing, tone, page flow, image style, or service presentation. They are less useful as a request to copy another company.

Also explain what you dislike about the current website or competitors. The goal is to define a direction suited to your audience and content, not to combine unrelated visual trends.

## Set the approval process

Name the decision-maker and decide how feedback will be collected. Consolidated feedback is easier to act on than separate messages from several stakeholders.

Agree on review stages, response times, revision limits, content deadlines, and what counts as approval. Late structural changes affect more than one screen, so important page and content decisions should happen before final polish.

## Agree on timeline and responsibilities

A timeline should show what both sides must deliver. Record who provides copy, images, legal text, access, feedback, technical setup, final approval, and launch communication.

Flag events, campaigns, domain changes, or internal deadlines that affect the launch. Keep time for testing. A website should not move from final design approval to public launch without responsive, technical, form, SEO, and content checks.

## Confirm launch and ongoing support

Before the project starts, decide who launches the website, takes backups, handles DNS, verifies forms, submits the sitemap, monitors errors, and responds if a problem appears.

Also clarify training, maintenance, updates, content publishing, hosting support, plugin licenses, and the support period after launch. These responsibilities are part of a professional handover.
