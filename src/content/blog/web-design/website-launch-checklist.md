A website can look finished and still be unready to launch. A missing form recipient, an old staging title, an unreadable mobile section, or an untracked conversion can turn launch day into an avoidable repair session.

This website launch checklist gives you a platform-neutral review process. Use it for a new business website, a redesign, a landing page, or a content-heavy site. The aim is not to chase perfection. It is to remove preventable problems before real visitors arrive.

## Confirm the strategy and launch goal

Start by checking what the website is supposed to achieve. A launch review becomes unfocused when the team only asks whether the pages “look good.”

- Confirm the primary audience.
- Confirm the main visitor action: inquiry, booking, purchase, signup, download, or visit.
- Check that the homepage explains the offer quickly.
- Verify that every important page has a clear next step.
- Confirm who approves the final launch.

Open the website as if you have never seen the business before. Within a few seconds, you should understand what it offers, who it serves, and what to do next. If that is unclear, fix the message before polishing small visual details.

## Review every piece of content

Content errors are highly visible after launch. Review the final website—not a separate document—because headings may wrap differently and text may be missing from the implemented version.

Check page titles, headings, paragraphs, button labels, contact details, opening hours, service information, prices, and legal text. Remove placeholder copy and internal notes. Confirm names, phone numbers, email addresses, and locations against an approved source.

Also check content structure:

- One descriptive H1 per page
- Logical H2 and H3 order
- Short, readable paragraphs
- Useful link text instead of repeated “click here” labels
- No duplicated sections left from a template
- No empty cards, categories, or author fields

## Check visual consistency

A page-by-page build can drift. Compare the final pages as one system.

Review typography sizes, button shapes, spacing, card padding, border treatments, icon style, image ratios, and section widths. Check that the primary action looks like the primary action everywhere. A visitor should not need to relearn the interface on each page.

Do not forget system pages such as search results, thank-you pages, empty states, 404 pages, and form confirmation messages.

## Test responsive layouts on real widths

Desktop approval is not a mobile review. Test several widths instead of dragging the browser once and assuming the layout works.

At minimum, review wide desktop, laptop, tablet, and small-phone views. Look for:

- Headings that create awkward single-word lines
- Navigation items that wrap or overlap
- Cards that become too narrow
- Images cropped around the wrong subject
- Buttons that overflow or become difficult to tap
- Tables and code blocks causing horizontal scrolling
- Fixed controls covering content
- Forms that are harder to complete on a phone

Test portrait and landscape orientation when the audience is likely to use tablets. Confirm that interactive targets have enough space and that keyboard focus remains visible.

## Complete the technical checks

Technical launch checks should verify how the site behaves, not just whether it loads once.

- Test internal links and important external links.
- Confirm the preferred domain and HTTPS version.
- Check redirects from old or changed URLs.
- Verify the favicon and web app icons.
- Remove staging banners, maintenance mode, and development-only scripts.
- Confirm custom fonts and third-party assets load successfully.
- Check the browser console for errors.
- Test a direct refresh on important routes.

If the project is a redesign, maintain a redirect map. A deleted URL should not silently become a 404 when a relevant replacement exists.

## Run the website launch SEO checklist

Search optimization begins with a site that can be crawled, understood, and indexed correctly.

Review each important page for a unique SEO title and meta description. Confirm the canonical URL, index settings, heading structure, internal links, descriptive image alt text, and clean URL slug. Make sure the production site is not carrying a staging `noindex` directive.

Then verify the wider system:

- XML sitemap uses the production domain.
- `robots.txt` does not block important pages or assets.
- Duplicate staging URLs are not indexable.
- Open Graph titles, descriptions, and images are suitable for sharing.
- Structured data matches visible content and contains no invented claims.
- Important pages can be reached through navigation or contextual links.

## Review performance and stability

Use a production-style build where possible. Development servers can hide the real size and behavior of the final assets.

Compress large images, use modern formats, and avoid serving a 3000-pixel file inside a small card. Add explicit image dimensions or aspect-ratio containers to reduce layout shifts. Lazy-load suitable below-the-fold images, but do not delay the main hero image unnecessarily.

Check the largest content element, layout shifts, and long tasks. Also test on a slower connection or mobile device. A website that feels fast on a designer’s workstation may behave differently for real visitors.

## Test forms and conversion paths

A working form is more than a visible submit button. Complete every important form from start to finish.

Confirm required fields, validation messages, keyboard behavior, success states, email delivery, spam protection, and the destination of each notification. Test links for phone calls, email, bookings, checkout, maps, and downloads.

Follow the main conversion path as a visitor:

1. Enter through the homepage or a service page.
2. Understand the offer.
3. Find proof or supporting information.
4. Reach the primary CTA.
5. Complete the action.
6. Receive a clear confirmation.

If the path contains a dead end or an unnecessary decision, simplify it before launch.

## Confirm legal and trust details

The exact requirements depend on the business and location, so obtain appropriate legal advice when needed. From a website-quality perspective, check that published policies are current, consent controls work as intended, and the business identity is clear.

Review privacy information, cookie behavior, terms, refund or cancellation information where relevant, copyright dates, image licenses, accessibility statements, and third-party disclosures. Do not publish copied placeholder policies.

Trust also comes from accurate contact information, honest claims, consistent branding, and clear ownership of the website.

## Verify analytics and search tools

If analytics is part of the project, confirm that it loads only after the required consent behavior. Test the key events that matter: form submissions, booking clicks, checkout clicks, phone calls, downloads, or other agreed actions.

Verify the production property, not a staging property. Check that internal traffic rules and referral handling are configured as expected. Connect the site to the appropriate search-console account and submit the production sitemap.

## Complete the final launch steps

Take a backup before changing the production environment. Record the current DNS, hosting, and deployment state so the team can recover if something unexpected happens.

Choose a launch window with enough time for observation. Publish the final version, clear relevant caches, confirm HTTPS, and rerun the most important checks on the live domain. Do not assume the production site matches staging until you have opened it yourself.

## Run post-launch checks

Review the site again immediately after launch, then after search engines, caches, and real visitors have had time to interact with it.

- Recheck forms and conversion events.
- Watch for 404s and redirect gaps.
- Review mobile behavior and real-device feedback.
- Check indexing and sitemap processing.
- Monitor performance and error logs.
- Confirm the client knows how to request support.

A launch checklist reduces risk, but a responsible launch also includes observation and handover.
